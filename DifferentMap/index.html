Exploring the use of Alcohol, Tobacco & other drugs in Australians

Exploring Drug Usage In Different States Of Australia

This choropleth map shows the percentage of people that smoke in different states of Australia. We can see Nothern Territory remains the top state for the number of daily smokers. People in their 40s and 50s are more likely to be smoking daily. There has been an increase in the proportion of adults who never take up smoking. There was 49% of people in 1991 that never smoked in comparison to 63% in 2019. Fun fact, the estimated social cost of tobacco use in 2015-16 was $136.7 billion.

This choropleth map explores the percentage of people who drink alcohol different states of Australia. Overall more than 8% of adults in Australia drink daily. Alcohol is the only drug with an approval rate for regular use higher than disapproval (45% approved and 21% disapproved). Alcohol was one of the most commonly detected substances, with higher consumption in regional areas than capital cities in 2020. n 2015-16 Australian households on average were spending $32 on alcoholic beverages per week.

In this choropleth map, we explore the percentage of people doing different drugs in Australia. In 2019, cannabis was one of the most commonly used illicit drugs in Australia. In 2019, more than 37% of people were using cannabis at least once a week. Cocaine use is increasing in Australia and is more common in people living in the major cities and areas with the highest socioeconomic advantages. In 2018-19, more than one-third of illicit drug seizures (34.0%) and arrests (30.0%) were for amphetamine-type substances.

Impact Of Drugs On Individual And Socity

In the bar chart, we explore the percentage of diseases that drinking alcohol led to in 2019. Almost 41 percent of people were likely to have liver cancer from drinking. The percentage rate of drink and driving in Australia is also high. More than 30% of fatal car accidents that occur in Australia have the involvement of alcohol. Alcohol is a dangerous drug as it also leads to a high level of violence. In 2018, alcohol was the fifth highest risk factor contributing to the burden of disease in Australia, 4.5% of the total load. Driving a motor vehicle is one of the riskiest activities people take when under the influence of alcohol (14.3% in 2019), which can be dangerous for the community.

Exploring Deaths From Drugs In Australia

The line chart shows the total death rate from all drugs in Australia. We can see a clear increasing trend in the current years as more new drugs are introduced to the public. In 2016, the deaths from drugs peaked, almost hitting 2000 deaths from drugs within one year. The death rate from using meth/amphetamine was four times more in 2019 than in 2000. There were 1317 deaths recorded from alcohol in 2019.

The line graph here explores the number of deaths different drugs have caused. The number of deaths has been steadily increasing since 2012. In recent years the deaths from drugs have been peaking. Over two-thirds (67%) of deaths relating to drugs in 2019 were accidental. However, more than one-quarter(24%) was intentional. The highest deaths due to drugs were of age 45-54 (15.1 deaths per 100,000 population) and closely followed by (14.2 deaths per 100,000 population) of age group from 35-44.
